Audiomesh

www.audiomesh.co

Audiomesh (formerly called Audiothrive) is a software project. As a musician, I noticed a gap in the mobile tools domain. Musicians resort to many different solutions for audio creation, sharing, storage, and sychronization. I started this side project in March 2014 and it continues today.

The Process

Filling A Need

I had unique insight into the problem of sharing audio as a musician and band member in several bands. It was always a hassle to share files, yes, even with services such as Google Drive. Drive is great, but getting the file to and from Drive is a hassle for audio recording. So I set out to create an app that does not require third party sharing services.

Validation

I began some background research to confirm or deny my suspicions. I created an online questionnaire. The most interesting finding was that, of those who responded, many indicated they had used a mobile device to record themselves while only 3 had not. This was interesting because my original idea years ago came at a time when mobile phones were not commonly used for such data intensive activities.

I also found that a decent amount of musicians collaborate with other musicians. Very few indicated otherwise.

There were other not so surprising findings and I collected a lot of demographic information. I decided to pursue the mobile idea and see how popular mobile audio collaboration really was.

I decided to look up all the bands I listen to and message them directly. I only asked one question: What do you use to get (musical) ideas down when you’re not in a studio? The results from this research confirmed the mobile theory. An overwhelming majority of artists indicated using their cell phone to pass around ideas. And some of these artists are pretty famous (7 million Facebook followers or more, certified platinum and gold albums). Others were garage bands.

PERSONAS & SCENARIOS

My ultimate goal was to cater to both the seasoned DIY musician, as well as someone who wouldn't know an audio mixer from a kitchen hand mixer. I am of the Don Norman mindset about personas, they are unnecessary in many situations because most users are the same. I had created personas for Audiomesh, but as I narrowed down my feature set, I abandoned them.

I also created 11 scenarios. Based on research results, I eliminated all but two of them.

WIREFRAMES & MOCKUPS

I iterated through hundreds of mockups. I had been mocking up UI since the research began, so as my findings came to light, I iterated on the wireframes until I narrowed them down to the most basic and useful tasks. I was also heavily influenced by The Lean Startup by Eric Reiss.

USABILITY TESTING

I chose a guerilla approach with my friends. Whenever I'm around a musician friend, I ask them to try the app. I have run about five people through parts of the UI with generally positive feedback.

Typically, people have an easy time recording a track, saving the sound and modifying the sharing settings. So far this is the only scenario I have tested. I used the app POP (Prototyping on Paper) to “paper” prototype using my Illustrator wire frames as well as tested actual code running on an iPhone.

OTHER METHODS

Card Sorting

During the first iteration of wire framing, I performed some card sorts with a couple of my friends. I had them prioritize and categorize different features in the app.

Development

I have been the sole UI developer on this project. I have been committing code to the online repository since March 30, 2014. I began learning Objective C when I started working on this project. My background until then consisted of a little Java experience through school-work and some apps I developed. I used Stack Overflow, a Stanford iOS7 online course, and the book iOS7 Programming Fundamentals by Matt Neuburg as my reference material.

I have written over 7,000 lines of code (I counted 14,000+ but that counts blank lines and comments, so I am estimating about half of that).

I have made over 160 commits to the Bit Bucket repository.

One large hurdle I overcame was the recording UI. I wanted something similar to Voice Memos. I scoured Stack Overflow, Github and every other site but no one seems to have replicated it. So I found a core graphics waveform view controller on github that did a few of the basic things I needed, but still lacked many important features such as scrolling through the waveform, showing the current time based on the scroll position, being able to overwrite the waveform visually (if I recorded over previous audio), etc. Also, if you recorded more than 5 seconds of audio, the performance of this waveform degraded until it was unnacceptable.

I was able to implement scrolling by having the core graphics draw on a UIScrollView rather than a UIView. As the user records, I widen the content view to match the speed of the waveform being drawn.

To overcome the performance degredation, I had to create my own method to loop through only the visible part of the waveform when redrawing. That way if I have five minutes of audio, it is actually only drawing about six seconds on the screen.

I implemented overwriting by matching the index in the audio data array to the position of the seeker (the point at which recording happens in the waveform). I was able to remove the existing point from the array and insert the new point which created an overwriting effect.

To a seasoned programmer these may or may not seem like big accomplishments, but since I am self-taught and many people online complained about wanting these features but provided no solutions, I was proud of what I achieved with a little determination.
